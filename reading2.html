WITH “AI SLOP” DISTORING OUR REALITY

Nesrine Malik’s article “AI slop” argues that AI-generated content is changing how people see and react to reality. Her main point is not just that fake images exist, but that the overwhelming mix of real and fake content makes it harder for people to care or respond seriously. When real news—such as war or political events—appears alongside AI-generated images, everything begins to feel equally artificial.

Malik suggests that this blurring of reality leads to emotional numbness. People stop reacting strongly even to real suffering because it becomes just another piece of content in a never ending feed. I agree with her argument because constant exposure to mixed media can make important events feel distant or unreal.

I also agree with Malik’s idea that AI content often reinforces certain biases, especially nostalgic or traditional ideals. Many AI-generated images show “perfect” families or outdated gender roles. This happens because AI systems are trained on past data, which already contains cultural bias.

Personally, I have also mistaken AI-generated images for real ones. As AI becomes more advanced, it gets harder to tell what is authentic. Sometimes when I see images or videos online, I genuinely question whether they are real or AI-generated. This uncertainty is becoming more common every day.

Social media platforms also contribute to the spread of AI slop. Companies like Facebook prioritize content that generates engagement, even if it is low quality or misleading. Because AI-generated content is cheap and easy to produce, it spreads quickly. The goal becomes attention, not truth, which keeps users endlessly scrolling instead of critically thinking.

AI content also spreads through private spaces like WhatsApp, where people are more likely to trust images shared by friends and family. This makes misinformation feel more personal and believable, increasing its impact.

Overall, Malik argues that AI slop does more than deceive—it overwhelms. When everything feels like content, even serious events lose their emotional weight. This creates a kind of numbness where people are aware of problems but no longer feel urgency to act. That is what she means by “sleepwalking into disaster.”
