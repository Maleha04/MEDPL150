MOTHERHOOD IN CRISIS

The article “Motherhood in Crisis” by Laurence Ivil, Alicia Prager, and Saidu Bah was a powerful and emotional article because it showed how dangerous pregnancy and childbirth can be for women in Sierra Leone. In many places, motherhood is seen as a joyful and natural part of life, but this article reminds readers that for some women, becoming a mother can mean risking their own lives.

It made me realize how unequal healthcare systems are around the world and how many women suffer daily simply because they do not have access to proper medical care. One part of the article that stood out was when it discussed how women had to travel long distances just to reach a clinic or hospital.

Some roads are difficult to travel on, especially in rural areas, and transportation is limited. This means women in labor may not get help in time. It is heartbreaking to think that something as natural as giving birth can become life-threatening because basic healthcare is too far away.

Reading about the four women’s personal stories also had a strong impact on me. Their experiences made the issue feel real and personal instead of just statistics. Each woman faced different struggles, but they all showed strength and courage while continuing to care for their children and families through fear, pain, and loss.

These stories remind readers that behind every number is a real person with emotions, dreams, and responsibilities. Another important issue discussed in the article is how crises like Ebola and COVID-19 made healthcare systems even weaker.

When countries faced epidemics, hospitals and clinics became overwhelmed. Resources were stretched thin, and pregnant women were often forgotten. This shows that women’s health is often pushed aside during emergencies, even though it should remain a priority. Mothers need care before, during, and after childbirth no matter what is happening in the world.

Some people around the world are fortunate to live in places with better healthcare systems, and many take hospitals, doctors, and emergency services for granted.

I now understand that healthcare systems are not equal everywhere. Women in Sierra Leone are not asking for luxury—they are asking for safe births, trained doctors, medicine, and a fair chance to survive. These are basic human needs.

In conclusion, “Motherhood in Crisis” is a sad and eye-opening article but also inspiring because of the resilience of the women who shared their stories. It teaches readers to appreciate healthcare access while recognizing global inequality. Most importantly, it reminds us that motherhood should never be a life-threatening experience—every woman deserves safety, dignity, and support when bringing a child into the world.
