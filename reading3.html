MOTHERHOOD IN CRISIS

Document

The article “Motherhood in Crisis” By Laurence Ivil, Alicia Prager, Saidu Bah was a powerful and emotional article I will say because it showed how dangerous pregnancy and childbirth can be for women in Sierra Leone. In many places motherhood is seen as a joyful and natural part of life, but this article reminds readers that for some women becoming a mother can mean risking their own lives. It made me realize that how unequal healthcare systems are around the world and how many women suffer daily simply because they do not have access to proper medical care. One part of the article that I found interesting was the section they talked about how women had to travel long distances just to reach a clinic or hospital. Some roads are difficult to travel on especially in rural areas and transportation is limited this means that women in labor may not get help in time. It is very heartbreaking to think that something as normal as giving birth can become life-threatening because basic healthcare is too far away. I will also say that reading about the four women’s personal stories also had an impact on me. There personal experiences made the issue feel real and very personal instead of just statistics. Each woman faced different struggles, but they all showed strength and courage. They all continued to move on through fear, pain and loss while trying to take care of their children and families. These four women’s stories remind readers that behind every number is a real person with emotions. Dreams, and responsibilities. Another important that was talked about in this article was how the crises like Ebola and COVID-19 made the healthcare systems even more weaker. When the counties faced epidemics, hospitals and clinics became very overwhelmed. Resources are stretched thin, and pregnant women may be forgotten. This just shows that women’s health is often pushed aside during emergencies, even though it should remain a priority. Mothers need care before, during and after childbirth no matter what else is happening in the world. Some people from around the world are very lucky to be able to live in places with better healthcare systems because many people take hospitals, doctors, and emergency services for granted. I understand now that healthcare systems are not always equal everywhere. Women in Sierra Leone are not asking for luxury they are asking for safe births, trained doctors, medicine, and fair chance to survive. These are basic human needs. In conclusion, “Motherhood in Crisis” was a sad and eye-opening story but also inspiring because of the resilience of the women who shared their personal stories. It teaches us readers to appreciate healthcare access while also recognizing global inequality. But most importantly it remind us all that motherhood should never be a life-threatening experience for any women every women in this world have the right to their own safety, dignity, and support when bringing a child into this world.
