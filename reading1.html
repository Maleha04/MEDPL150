In Plato's Cave by Susan Sontag

The reading “In Plato’s Cave” by Susan Sontag feels unsettling because it does not just criticize photography as a medium, but exposes how deeply images control the way we experience reality. Reading this essay in a world dominated by social media platforms like TikTok and Instagram makes her argument feel even more relevant. Sontag suggests that photography trains us to see the world as a collection of images rather than lived experiences.

I have noticed this in my own life—moments often do not feel complete unless they are photographed, posted, or saved. It is almost as if the experience only becomes real once it has been turned into an image. What stood out to me is Sontag’s claim that photography gives us the illusion of knowledge and participation while actually distancing us from reality.

When scrolling through social media, I see endless images of protests, war, luxury vacations, and people’s lifestyles. A video of suffering appears between a meme and an outfit post. Sontag’s discussion of photographing suffering feels especially relevant today. Images of violence and tragedy circulate widely, often without context, and I sometimes feel guilty consuming them passively.

Photography can awaken conscience but cannot sustain it without political and historical understanding. I agree with this—images alone rarely push me to act. Instead, they often leave me overwhelmed or numb. Constant exposure makes suffering feel both real and distant at the same time.

However, I do not fully agree with everything Sontag argues. Photographs can preserve memory, reveal injustice, and give visibility to people who might otherwise be ignored. Still, I agree that simply seeing something is not the same as understanding it. Liking or sharing an image can feel like engagement, but it often replaces deeper reflection or action.

This reading made me reflect on how I interact with images and what I expect them to do for me. Sontag shows that photographs are powerful but limited—they cannot explain, judge, or truly teach on their own. Understanding requires context, time, and narrative, not just images. In an image-saturated world, resisting the urge to capture everything may be one of the few ways left to step out of the cave and experience reality more honestly.
