The reading “In Plato’s Cave” by Susan Sontag feels unsetting because this reading does not just criticize photography as a medium but also exposes how deeply images control the way we experience reality. Reading this essay in a world which is being dominated by social media platforms like TikTok, and Instagram makes her argument feel realistic.Sontag suggests that photography trains us to see the world as a collection of images rather than lived experiences. I am also noticed this in my own personal life that often moments do not feel complete unless they are being photographed, posted, or being saved. It’s like as if the experience only becomes real once it has been turned into an image of some sort.What stood out to me about this reading is that Sontag saying that photography gives us the illusion of knowledge and participation while it is distancing us from the reality. When I am scrolling through social media I see endless number of images about protests, war, luxury vacations, bodies, and people’s lifestyles. A video of suffering appears between a meme and an outfit post.Sontag’s discussion of photographing suffering is especially relevant today. Images of violence or tragedy circulate widely, often without context and I sometimes feel guilty for consuming them passively. Photography can awaken conscience but cannot sustain it without political and historical understanding and I agree with this because images alone rarely push me to act. They have often left me feeling overwhelmed or numb. The constant exposure makes suffering feel both real and distant at the same time. But I would also like to say that I don’t fully agree with everything the way Sontag is describing photographs. Photographs can preserve memory, reveal injustice and give visibility to people who might other be ignored. However,I do agree with Sontag that just simply seeing something is not the same as truly understanding it. Taking a picture, liking a post, or sharing an image can feel like an engagement, but it often replaces a deeper reflection or action. The camera then becomes a way to avoid discomfort rather than confronting it.'In Plato’s Cave' this reading is making me think how I interact with images and what I expect them to do for me. Sontag makes it clear that photographs are powerful but limited they cannot explain, judge, or truly teach on their own. Understanding it requires context, time, and narrative not just images. In an image saturated world resisting the urge to turn everything into a photograph may be one of the few ways left to step of the cave and actually experience the reality more honestly.
